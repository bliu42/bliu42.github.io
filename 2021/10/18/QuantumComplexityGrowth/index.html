论文：Models of Quantum Complexity Growth

发表于 2021-10-18 分类于 科研笔记 阅读次数：

Brandão, F. G. S. L., Chemissany, W., Hunter-Jones, N., Kueng, R., & Preskill, J. (2021). Models of Quantum Complexity Growth. PRX Quantum, 2(3), 1. https://doi.org/10.1103/prxquantum.2.030316

The paper provides a relation between the approximate designs and the quantum complexity.

Definition of the Quantum Complexity

Def 1 (Strong State Complexity): The complexity of a quantum state $|\psi \rangle$ is the minimal circuit size required to implement an ancilla-assisted measurement that is capable of distinguishing $\rho = |\psi\rangle \langle \psi|$ from the maximally mixed state $\rho_0 = (1/d) \mathbb{I}$.

Pictographic illustration:

Black box: $\rho$ or $\rho_0$.
Blue boxes: preprocessing circuits involving $r$ 2-local gates.

We say that the state has complexity less than $r$ if the probability of correctly distinguishing both states is close to optimal.
The optimal: $\beta_{QC}^# (r,|\psi \rangle) \to 1-\frac{1}{d}$.
$|\psi \rangle$ has strong $\delta$ complexity iff $\beta_{QC}^# (r,|\psi \rangle)\geq 1-\frac{1}{d} -\delta$. Denote: $\mathcal{C}_\delta(|\psi \rangle)\leq r$.

Def 2 (Strong Unitary Complexity): The complexity of a quantum circuit U is the minimal circuit size required to implement an ancilla-assisted measurement that is capable of distinguishing $\rho \to \mathcal{U}(\rho) = U\rho U^\dagger$ from the completely depolarizing channel $\rho \to \mathcal{D}(\rho) = (1/d)\mathbb{I}$.

Pictographic illustration:

Black box: $U$ or $\mathbb{I}$ operation on $\rho$.
Blue boxes: pre- and postprocessing circuits involving $r’$ and $r’’$ 2-local gates.

We say that the unitary has complexity less than $r = r’+r’’$ if the probability of correctly distinguishing both options is close to optimal.
The optimal: $\beta_{QC}^# (r,U) \to \frac{1}{2} |\mathcal U -\mathcal{D}|{\diamond} = 1-\frac{1}{d^2}, r \to \infty$.
$U$ has strong $\delta$ complexity iff $\beta{QC}^# (r,U)\geq 1-\frac{1}{d^2} -\delta$. Denote: $\mathcal{C}_\delta(U)\leq r$.

Complexity by Design

Theorem 1 (State Complexity Growth): Consider the set of (pure) states in $d = q^n$ that results from applying all unitaries in an $\epsilon$-approximate 2k-design toe a fixed (but random) initial state $|\psi_0 \rangle$. Then the set contains at least

$$
\left(\begin{array}{c}
d+k-1 \
k
\end{array}\right)\left[\frac{1}{1+\epsilon}-2 d(n+1)^{r}|\mathbf{G}|^{r}\left(\frac{16 k^{2}}{d(1-\delta)^{2}}\right)^{k}\right]
$$

distinct states that obey $\mathcal{C}_\delta(|\psi \rangle ) \geq r+1$ each.
Qualitatively, the number is $\mathcal{O}[(d/k)^k]$ as long as r obeys $r \lesssim \frac{k[n-2 \log (k)]}{\log (n)}$.

Theorem 2 (Unitary Complexity Growth): A discrete approximate 2k-design in $d=q^n$ dimensions contains at least

$$
\frac{d^{2 k}}{k !}\left[\frac{1}{1+\epsilon}-3 d^{2} n^{2 r}|\mathbf{G}|^{r}\left(\frac{1024 k^{4}}{d(1-\delta)^{2}}\right)^{k}\right]
$$

distinct states that obey $\mathcal{C}_\delta(U) \geq r+1$ each.
Qualitatively, the number is $\mathcal{O}[(d^2/k)^k]$ as long as r obeys $r \lesssim \frac{k[n-4 \log (k)]}{\log (n)}$.
